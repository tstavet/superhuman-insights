What the split shows

The two lead sections carry the email. Trending Socials (42.6%) and Today in AI (26.8%) are the engine. From the Frontier, ICYMI, and Trending Tools cluster in the 7–8% range, each pulling its weight for its size. Ads sit at 5.9%.

Two sections need a different lens

Prompt Station shows zero clicks because it has no links — the prompts are copy-paste text. It can't be judged by clicks at all, only by comments, where it's the single most-praised feature (72 mentions). Don't read the zero as failure; it's unmeasurable by this method. And The AI Academy's low 1.6% is partly structural: some issues run the tutorial as a sponsored placement, so its clicks route through the Ads bucket.

Method note: "The Friday Four" weekly recap is folded into From the Frontier, since it occupies the same structural slot. Sample skews slightly toward recent sends. A link appearing under two sections across different issues is counted under each. Full 30-send processing would tighten these figures but the ranking is stable.

01 / THE HEADLINE

Two sections carry the newsletter. Everything else is a rounding error on clicks.

Across all 30 sends, Trending Socials and Editorial Outbound together take 85% of every click. The rest of the email — resources, course, referral, author socials — competes for the remaining 15%, and most of it lands under 4%.

Metric: verified unique email clicks, summed across 30 sends. "Verified" strips bot and prefetch clicks (raw totals overstate low-engagement links by up to 2×). List-management links — unsubscribe, preference, and poll URLs, one per recipient — are excluded from the ranking as infrastructure, not content.

02 / LENGTH

Longer sends earn more total clicks, but each item works less hard.

Editions ranged from 17 to 37 content links (median 26). The data splits the "is longer worse?" question in two, and the answer is different for each half.

+0.41
Correlation between content links and total clicks. More items, more raw clicks.
−0.34
Correlation between content links and clicks per item. More items, thinner attention on each.
17–37
Content-link range per send. The shortest sends were among the highest per-item performers.

This is diminishing returns, not collapse. Adding a section grows the raw total a little while spreading reader attention thinner across the whole email. The shortest sends in the set — the 17-link "China closes the AI gap" and the 22-link "Apple sues OpenAI" — sit at the top of per-item engagement (877 and 939 clicks per content link), well above the 45-link video-editor edition.

Takeaway

The goal isn't "shorter for its own sake." It's fewer, better items. Cutting the weakest 20% of links per send should cost almost no total clicks (they were barely clicked) while lifting attention on what remains. Test a hard cap near the current short end: roughly 22–26 content links.

03 / CLUTTER & READABILITY

Readers can't always tell the newsletter from the ads.

The clutter problem isn't only length. It's signal-to-noise, and readers name it directly. Sponsor content pulls real clicks (11,180 across 30 sends, 2.4%), but a recurring complaint is that ad density makes the whole email feel like a skim.

It was okIt is hard to tell how much of your newsletter is advertising, so I am inclined to do a much much lighter skim.
It was okA lot of marketing smoke.
TerribleIt does not fit my computer screen. / Your email is not displaying in its entirety.

Two separable fixes surface here: label sponsored content unambiguously (so trust in the editorial rises rather than bleeding into the ads), and audit the mobile render — a handful of readers report the email cutting off, which at scale is silent unsubscribes.

Takeaway

Only 1 of 30 sends had a sponsor link as its single most-clicked item, so ads aren't cannibalizing attention on the numbers. The problem is perceived density. A clear, consistent "Presented by" treatment and one fewer promo block per send buys back trust without costing sponsor revenue.

04 / SECTION MIX

Keep, fix, cut, test.

What the click and comment data jointly support, held against each section's job in the email.

Keep — these are the engine

Trending Socials (43.5%) and Editorial news (41.7%) are the reasons people open. Don't touch the core. But see the caution below on how the socials link out.

Fix — loved in theory, ignored in practice

The Prompts/Tools pack is the single most-praised content in the comments (72 mentions, the #1 positive theme) yet the click-out to it draws just 1.1% of clicks. That gap is the biggest opportunity in the email: readers value prompts, but the CTA to the full pack isn't converting. The pack is buried, stale, or both.

Loved itSuper helpful prompts, examples and advice in this issue. Very usable!
It was okTried to delve into the 100 prompts for ChatGPT. Yeah right — dragged through pages…

Cut or shrink — near-zero return

Author social links in the footer drew 448 clicks total across 30 sends (0.1%), averaging under 4 clicks per link. Advertise-with-us CTA is similar at 0.1%. These aren't earning their line space in a newsletter fighting length.

Test — reader-requested, currently absent

Two experiments the comments openly ask for: a beginner track or glossary (acronyms are a named barrier), and a weekly digest option for readers who find daily too much. Both target churn directly (see §5).

Caution — a fixable revenue-and-trust leak

Multiple readers can't use your X / Twitter links — X now blocks logged-out viewing on mobile, so a chunk of your audience hits a wall on your single biggest section. One reader: "X posts are not articles. If you say you're linking to an article, link to an article." Where a primary source exists, link the source, not the tweet.

05 / RE-ENGAGING LAPSED & OLDER READERS

The people rating it "Terrible" aren't bored. They're lost.

This is the clearest signal in the comment set, and it runs opposite to the usual "make it punchier" instinct. The dissatisfaction clusters around technical depth outrunning a large, loyal, non-expert audience — many of whom self-identify as older or new to AI.

6.6%

It was okThe whole article is getting too complicated for my understanding. I'm still back at basic concepts.
Loved itI'm a boomer non-techie, and I really like this newsletter. Thank you for keeping it free.

The last quote matters as much as the complaints: this audience is devoted when the newsletter meets them. They're not asking you to dumb it down across the board. They're asking for a way in — defined acronyms on first use, an occasional "basics" explainer, a plain-language line under the dense items.

Takeaway

Define every acronym on first use (a house-style rule, near-zero cost). Add a recurring one-line "in plain terms" gloss to the densest story. Pilot a beginner-oriented section or a "start here" onboarding email for new subscribers — several first-time readers said they felt "way behind" with no entry point.

Takeaway — upgrade the magnets, don't just relink them

The prompts pack draws praise but one reader called navigating it a slog ("dragged through pages"). The value is real; the delivery is dated. Rebuild the pack as a searchable, categorized resource (one reader literally asked, "is there a way to search the newsletter's database for articles on a subject?"), refresh the tools list on a schedule, and give the CTA a clearer promise than a bare link. A loved-but-unclicked asset is a conversion problem, not a content problem.

08 / METHOD & LIMITS

What this is built on, and what it can't say.

Sources. 30 per-send click exports (verified unique email clicks + CTR, links tagged by utm_campaign); one deliverability export (opens by recipient domain, 594k recipients); 640 deduplicated reader comments with Loved it / OK / Terrible ratings.

Classification. Links were bucketed by URL structure and UTM signature into Trending Socials, Editorial Outbound, Sponsor Ads, three Resource types, Author Socials, and List Management. Fine-grained editorial splits (Today in AI vs. Prompt Station vs. Tools) are not reliably recoverable from click data alone — the same domains recur across sections and link order doesn't track email position — so editorial is reported as one bucket rather than guessed.

Limits, stated plainly. Comments carry no send tag, so sentiment is pooled across 30 days, not attributed per edition. The open-rate split is one send and the audience-type mapping is a proxy, so it's directional. Click data measures link engagement, not readership — a section people read and don't click looks weak here, which is why editorial judgment, not clicks alone, drives the "keep" calls. Vocal commenters skew to the delighted and the annoyed; theme direction is trustworthy, exact proportions less so.
